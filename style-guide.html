Part I

Content & Voice

Every sentence on the site delivers one piece of condensed, accurate information. These rules are the standard each briefing, walk note, theme page, and crux box is edited against.

The register — encyclopedia, not newsletter

The copy is an objective summary, not commentary. Its purpose is to share condensed information, not to persuade, react, or perform. The default register is an encyclopedia entry, written for a reader who wants the idea rather than the writer's opinion of it.

  1. Plain declarative sentences. Front-load the claim. State what the source says, then stop.
  2. No editorializing. No winks, no nonchalance, no evaluative flourish. The information is the point; the writer is not in the frame.
  3. Attribute what's contested. When a claim is one person's estimate or argument, name whose it is — "Carlsmith estimates," "the paper argues" — so the reader can weigh it.
  4. Compress ruthlessly, but never past the truth. A vivid three-word compression is welcome if it's factually exact. Shorter is better only until it starts to distort.
  5. Neutral on live disagreements. Where serious people disagree, the site lays out the disagreement (that's what crux boxes are for) rather than quietly picking a side.
Tone & attitude, in one line Calm, informative, unaffiliated. The numbers and the arguments carry the weight — not adjectives, not urgency, not a house opinion. The site summarizes the debate; it does not enter it.

Banned patterns and kept exceptions

The running verdicts from the site's revision passes: patterns removed wherever they appear, and the exceptions kept because they are both vivid and accurate.

Banned
  • Evaluative adverbs & superlatives
    strikingly, landmark, rigorous, "strongest available"
  • Casual idioms
    punt, tap out, "& co.", "back in 2020"
  • Insinuating adverbs
    "quietly assume," "conveniently"
  • Generic-you
    "a utility you can point at problems"
  • Newsletter framing
    "The takeaway is…," "Here's the thing"
Allowed
  • First-person plural — "we/us" is allowed where it mirrors the literature's own framing, never as reader-address.
  • Vivid-but-factual compression
    "Genius as a utility."
  • "nuclear-plant territory" — a real scale reference, not hype.
  • "random SF startup" — accurate, and does work no formal phrase would.
  • "worth fighting for" — kept on the Positive-futures theme; it's the sources' own stance.
Corrections — three recurring kinds of edit

These are representative before/after edits from the site's revision history, grouped into the three kinds of correction that recur most often.

1. De-editorializing — the same fact, stated plainly

Voice · from the site-wide de-editorializing pass
Cut"…and everything else follows from taking it seriously."
Kept"The rest of the paper works out the consequences."
Why: the first version editorializes — it tells the reader how to feel about the premise. The second just describes the paper's structure. Same information, without steering the reader.
Voice · generic-you removed, compression kept
Cut"a utility you can point at problems"
Kept"Genius as a utility."
Why: "you can point at" is generic-you and reads conversational. The compressed noun phrase keeps the vivid idea and drops the address.

2. De-fabricating — cutting anything the source never said

Fabrication · caught in the accuracy audit
Cut — inventedKarnofsky was "sweating profusely" over the century.
Kept — real quote"…we should be doing a double take" at the century we live in.
Why: the vivid phrase was never in the source — it was fabricated color. It was replaced with the writer's actual words. On a subject where people disagree, a fabricated quote is a serious failure.
Fabrication · an entire argument removed
Cut — inventedCotra's "substitution-vs-adoption" argument (attributed to reading 6).
Kept — what's thereBack-loaded impacts; uses that aren't gated by legitimacy. Bio-anchors framed as background, not the dialogue.
Why: the substitution-vs-adoption line wasn't in the piece at all. Deleted, not softened.

3. Precision — right claim, wrong scope or detail

Precision · scope tightened
CutErdil: ~20-year median to full automation of "the economy."
Kept…full automation of remote work.
Why: "the economy" over-claims the source. Remote work is what the estimate is actually about.
Precision · attribution corrected
CutAschenbrenner calls for the "nationalization" of AI labs.
Kept"The Project" — which he explicitly disclaims is nationalization.
Why: the source pre-empts exactly this misreading. Getting an author's own claim right is non-negotiable.
Precision · number and evaluation both fixed
CutCarlsmith puts existential catastrophe at ">10%"; MacAskill ranks the "most serious / most underrated" risks.
KeptCarlsmith's multiplied estimate is ~5% (the >10% is his later 2022 holistic revision); severity rankings removed entirely.
Why: two errors in one region — a number pinned to the wrong version of the estimate, and superlative rankings the source never made.
The accuracy rule

One rule sits beneath the voice: every content claim traces to a source, and any new claim is verified against that source before it ships. A full audit — eleven parallel checks against the readings — produced about sixty corrections; the examples above are from it. Two standing consequences follow:

Standing rules Never claim a relationship the map doesn't draw. A walk note may not assert two ideas are "in tension" unless a tension edge actually connects them. And no invented color — no quote, metaphor, or figure that isn't in the source, however good it would sound.

Part II

Visual System

The visual system serves the same goal as the voice: legible and unobtrusive. Light, editorial, hand-rolled — one accent color, two fonts, borders instead of shadows. This part is the condensed reference; the fuller version lives in the design-language spec the site was distilled into.

Principles

The site is built to be easy to move through and easy to reverse. The principles behind that:

Navigation
  • Everything on the web is clickable — every concept and every theme opens its own page.
  • Navigation is always reversible — HOME and BACK are both always present, and Escape or a double-click resets the view.
  • Hover to explore before committing — hovering a concept or source highlights its region of the web, so you can look before you click.
  • No dead ends — any path can be walked back, so exploring never has a cost.

The one hover cue — copy it exactly

Rest-state color says what a thing is. Hover says "you're on it" via intensity only: the element's own border thickens 1px→2px, and padding drops 1px to compensate — zero layout shift. Never a hue change on hover; never filter:brightness() (it muddies inner white badges). Try the button:

concept chip WEBMATRIX 
.thing        { border:1px solid var(--ctrl); padding:7px 12px; }
.thing:hover  { border-width:2px;             padding:6px 11px; }

Border strength encodes meaning: --line for dividers and non-interactive surfaces; --ctrl (ink 45%) for pressable controls; theme-85% / ink-60% for content pills, the strongest borders on the page. Segmented controls use a raised white pill for the active tab — never an underline (underline tabs read "vibecoded").
